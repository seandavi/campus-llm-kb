### 1. Argument spine

The arrival of Large Language Models (LLMs) in the academic medical center has fundamentally disrupted the "product-based" assessment model of health professions education. Initial institutional responses were largely reactive, centering on prohibition through honor code updates and the adoption of AI detection tools—a "failed framing" due to the technical unreliability of detectors and their disproportionate impact on non-native speakers. A more robust and workable framing treats LLMs as a permanent feature of the clinical and academic landscape, shifting pedagogy from grading final outputs (essays, clinical notes) to evaluating the reasoning process itself. Because health professions students must graduate with the ability to safely oversee AI-augmented care, "AI literacy" must be integrated as a core competency rather than treated as an elective or administrative hurdle. The chapter concludes that the stakes of academic integrity in medical education are uniquely high: the outsourcing of clinical reasoning to a "black box" without oversight represents a direct threat to patient safety, requiring a shift toward authentic, simulation-based assessments that prioritize human-in-the-loop accountability.

### 2. Section outline

1.  **The Collapse of the Proxy:** Argues that traditional written assessments (take-home essays, case writeups) served as proxies for student reasoning that are now irrevocably broken by LLMs.
2.  **The Detection Trap:** Critiques the prohibitionist response, detailing why AI detection tools fail technically and ethically, citing the FTC's regulatory posture on "AI-washing."
3.  **Tiered Integration:** Proposes a "workable framing" of tiered course policies (Prohibited, Permitted with Disclosure, Integrated) that aligns with writing-studies scholarship and clinical reality.
4.  **Beyond the USMLE:** Examines the "Step Exam Paradox"—where AI can pass the boards but cannot yet replicate clinical judgment—and the need for competency-based medical education (CBME) reform.
5.  **Defining AI Literacy:** Synthesizes the AAMC (2024) and AMIA competency frameworks into a curriculum for "AI commissioners" rather than just "AI consumers."
6.  **Simulation and Authenticity:** Argues for moving assessment into "un-bot-able" spaces, such as SP-LLM (Standardized Patient LLM) simulations and oral "viva" examinations of clinical reasoning.
7.  **Institutional Benchmarking:** Provides a comparative analysis of how leading medical schools (UCSF, Harvard, Mayo) have moved from generic university policies to health-specific AI governance.
8.  **The Safety Mandate:** Reconceptualizes academic integrity as a patient-safety issue, framing the development of independent clinical judgment as an ethical imperative in an augmented age.

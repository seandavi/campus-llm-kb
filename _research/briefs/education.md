# Chapter brief: Education Domain

**Target file:** `resources_education.qmd`
**Status:** currently a curated link list — needs to become a book chapter.
**Length target:** ≥6 rendered pages (~3,000–4,500 words of body prose).
**Position in book:** first domain chapter, under the "Domain Resources" part.

## Scope

Education at an academic medical center: medical school, graduate programs,
nursing education, health professions education, GME/residency, continuing
medical education, and faculty development. **Not scope:** K-12, non-health
higher education in general — though relevant cross-sector evidence is welcome
as supporting evidence.

## Argument the chapter should make

The academic medical center's response to generative AI in education has
largely been reactive and policy-first — honor-code updates and detection-tool
purchases — when the deeper challenge is pedagogical: how to teach competencies
that remain meaningful when AI can pass the USMLE and write a competent clinical
note. The chapter should move the reader from "what do we do about ChatGPT
cheating?" to "what does AI-era health professions education actually look like?"

## Areas the dossier must cover

- **What the arrival of LLMs actually disrupted**: the specific assessment
  types that failed first (take-home essays, open-ended clinical case writeups,
  literature reviews), the empirical studies of student use by 2024–2025, and
  faculty response patterns. Cite survey data where it exists.
- **The two failed framings and why they failed**:
  - The prohibition framing: honor code updates, detection tools (Turnitin AI,
    GPTZero), and the evidence for why they are unreliable and inequitable.
    Cite the FTC's posture on AI detection and the academic literature on
    false-positive rates.
  - The laissez-faire framing: "just let students use it" without redesigning
    assessment — what happens to learning outcomes.
- **A workable framing**: tiered course policies (prohibited, permitted with
  disclosure, permitted without restriction), transparent disclosure norms
  for students, assessment redesign moving from product-grading to
  process-grading. Cite the writing-studies scholarship (Mollick at Wharton,
  Anson on authorship, the WAC/WID community).
- **Medical-education-specific stakes**: the USMLE and board exam context
  (GPT-4 passing Step 1–3 — cite the empirical papers); competency-based
  medical education frameworks (ACGME milestones, EPA frameworks) and what AI
  changes about how we assess them; clinical reasoning assessment in the
  simulation lab.
- **AI literacy as curriculum content**: what medical students and residents
  should know about AI systems by graduation. Cite the AAMC 2024 AI
  competencies report if published; cite McCoy et al. 2020 (NPJ Digital
  Medicine) and James/Wachter 2022 (JAMA) on what clinicians need to know.
  The difference between "AI consumer" literacy and "AI commissioner" literacy.
- **Faculty development**: the majority of faculty have had no training in
  AI-era pedagogy; the institutional responsibility; models from peer
  institutions; what a faculty development program looks like.
- **Institutional policy survey**: what leading medical schools and health
  professions schools have published (not just generic universities — find
  health-professions-specific policies from UCSF, Johns Hopkins, Mayo Clinic,
  Harvard Medical School, AAMC guidance). Build toward a comparative table.
- **Academic integrity in health professions context**: the stakes are higher
  than in general higher ed — a physician who learned to outsource reasoning
  to AI without understanding is a patient safety issue. Frame that
  specifically.

## Figure and table targets

- A Mermaid flowchart: "Assessment redesign decision tree" — takes an
  instructor through: what is the learning objective? → is AI use realistic
  in practice? → what assessment mode matches? → what disclosure/policy tier?
- A table of institutional AI policies from five or more health-professions
  schools/programs: institution, scope, tier system used, key prohibitions,
  disclosure requirements, date. Find the actual policy documents.
- A table of AI literacy competency frameworks proposed for health professions
  education (AAMC, AMIA, individual papers) — compare what each proposes
  students should know.

## Voice constraints

Directed at medical education leadership (deans, clerkship directors, course
directors, GME program directors) and faculty. Should be practical and
specific — not a general AI-in-education survey, but one calibrated to the
particular pressures and standards of health professions education. Should
resist both technophobia and techno-utopianism.

## Open questions to flag

- AAMC has been producing AI guidance documents; locate the most current ones
  and note their publication dates.
- LCME accreditation standards — flag whether they have addressed AI yet.
- The empirical literature on student AI use in medical education specifically
  is growing fast; distinguish pre-print from peer-reviewed findings.

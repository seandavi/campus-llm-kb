## What is MISSING and must be researched

### Gap 1: Empirical evidence for the AI literacy gap
The current chapter makes a strong Dunning-Kruger claim ("clinicians consistently overestimate their AI knowledge") without a specific citation. Find the actual validated assessment studies — what instruments have been used to assess AI literacy in healthcare workers? What do the scores show? Look for studies using the AI Literacy Scale (AILS), MAILS, or similar validated instruments applied to medical students, residents, nurses, or faculty.

### Gap 2: ACGME and LCME requirements
Do ACGME program requirements now mention AI literacy or AI safety as a competency? Which specialty boards have added AI-related content to their milestones? Has LCME added AI literacy to its standards for medical education? Has COCA (for osteopathic programs) or CCNE/ACEN (for nursing) issued guidance?

### Gap 3: Shadow AI prevalence data
The chapter asserts that administrative staff are the highest-risk group for shadow AI but doesn't cite empirical data. Are there published surveys of shadow AI use in AMC settings? What percentage of AMC employees report using consumer AI tools for work tasks? Any data on frequency of PHI exposure via consumer AI?

### Gap 4: Sustaining the program: half-life of AI training
AI capabilities change faster than CME/CNE update cycles. What does a "living curriculum" program for AI literacy look like? Are there peer institutions that have built programs with continuous update mechanisms? What is the CME/CNE credit infrastructure for AI literacy modules — which major society conferences are offering AI CME?

### Gap 5: Measuring AI literacy over time
The chapter doesn't address how the institution measures whether training worked. What assessment approaches are used by peer AMCs? Is there a standard for "AI literacy proficiency" analogous to BLS certification?

## Scope

Building the human capacity to understand and responsibly use generative AI
across the academic medical center — staff, clinicians, researchers, trainees,
faculty, and leadership. **Not scope:** undergraduate or graduate curriculum
design (that is the Education domain chapter); data governance or
infrastructure (separate chapters).

## Argument the chapter should make

AI literacy at an AMC is not a one-time training event; it is a capability
the institution must build into its hiring, onboarding, continuing education,
and leadership development at every level. The chapter should give the CHRO,
CMO, CNO, and dean's office a framework for what that capability looks like,
why a single "AI awareness" module is insufficient, and what a sustained
workforce development program requires.

## Areas the dossier must cover

- **Competency tiers**: the classic three-tier AI competency framework for
  healthcare (consumer, translator, developer — cite Ng 2023, Cell Reports
  Medicine) applied to the AMC workforce. What each tier needs to know. A
  fourth tier: leadership/governance (board, C-suite, department chairs). The
  different stakes at each tier.
- **Competency frameworks in the literature**: AAMC AI competencies for
  medical students and residents; AMIA's workforce development positions;
  ACGME milestone mapping to AI-related competencies; nursing and allied
  health equivalents; the WHO's Health Workforce guidance on AI. Find the
  most current primary documents from each body with publication dates.
- **Role-specific training needs**:
  - Clinicians: clinical decision support literacy, when to trust/override AI,
    documentation of AI-assisted decisions, consent conversations with patients.
  - Researchers: research integrity with AI (authorship, peer review, data
    use), prompt engineering for research tasks, evaluating AI-generated
    literature summaries.
  - Administrative staff: data classification, safe use of enterprise AI
    tools, recognizing shadow-IT risks.
  - IT and informatics staff: model evaluation, RAG system maintenance,
    monitoring deployed AI.
  - Leadership and governance: what to ask vendors, how to read a model card,
    governance roles and responsibilities.
- **Training modalities and delivery**: the evidence base for e-learning vs.
  simulation vs. peer learning for AI competency development; what peer
  institutions have deployed; the role of CME/CNE credit; micro-credentialing
  and badging; the train-the-trainer model for scale.
- **The faculty development gap**: faculty responsible for teaching AI-related
  competencies often lack them themselves; what an AMC faculty development
  program looks like; examples from peer institutions.
- **Measuring competency**: how you know whether training worked; assessment
  instruments for AI literacy (are any validated?); the difference between
  awareness, competence, and proficiency; KPIs at the workforce level.
- **Hiring and onboarding**: what AI literacy requirements look like in job
  descriptions; how to assess AI competency in interviews; what onboarding
  should cover for new AMC employees regardless of role.
- **Sustaining the program**: AI is changing fast; the half-life of any
  specific training is short; how to build a program that updates itself;
  the role of an internal AI community of practice; partnerships with
  professional societies and academic programs.

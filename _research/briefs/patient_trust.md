# Chapter brief: Patient and Community Trust

**Target file:** `patient_trust.qmd` (new file)
**Length target:** ≥6 rendered pages (~3,000–4,500 words).
**Position in book:** second chapter of the "Agentic AI in Practice" part.

## Scope

The social license for AI in healthcare — the degree to which patients and
the communities served by an AMC actually trust AI systems in their care —
and the institutional mechanisms for building and maintaining it. This is
distinct from the regulatory compliance and ethics chapters. Compliance
answers "are we allowed to?" Social license answers "do the people we serve
believe we should?"

## Argument the chapter should make

The institutions that deploy AI in healthcare successfully in the long term
are not the ones that moved fastest or had the most sophisticated technology.
They are the ones that brought patients and communities along. Social license
is not a constraint on AI deployment — it is a precondition for it being
effective. A clinical AI tool that patients distrust will produce worse
outcomes than one that earns trust, because trust affects whether patients
engage honestly with AI-assisted care, follow AI-informed recommendations,
and return for follow-up. An AMC that deploys AI without understanding its
community's trust landscape is flying blind.

## Areas the dossier must cover

- **The empirical trust landscape**: what published surveys actually show about
  patient and community attitudes toward AI in healthcare in 2024–2026.
  Distinguish by: demographic group (race, ethnicity, age, insurance status,
  chronic condition); type of AI application (diagnosis support vs. ambient
  documentation vs. patient-facing chatbot vs. administrative automation);
  and who is doing the deploying (trusted clinician, anonymous health system,
  AI company). Cite the AMA patient surveys, Pew Research data, and any
  published peer-reviewed survey studies.
- **Why trust is not uniform**: the historical context for differential trust
  — Tuskegee and its documented multi-generational effects on medical research
  participation, the history of medical experimentation on marginalized
  communities, contemporary data showing Black and Latino patients report lower
  trust in AI diagnostic tools. This is not background material to acknowledge
  and move past; it is the explanation for why community engagement must be
  genuine rather than performative.
- **Informed consent for AI in care**: what existing consent frameworks cover
  and do not cover when AI is involved; the specific gaps for ambient
  documentation (the patient is being recorded), predictive risk scoring
  (invisible to the patient), and AI-generated clinical notes (the patient may
  not know the note was AI-drafted); what "meaningful" disclosure looks like
  vs. boilerplate.
- **Patient AI Advisory Councils**: what they are, how they differ from
  standard patient advisory boards, who should be on them, how they interact
  with the AISC, what authority they have. Find examples from institutions that
  have established them — this is an emerging governance structure with a small
  but growing evidence base.
- **Community engagement for AI governance**: the difference between
  informing the community and involving the community in governance decisions;
  what participatory design looks like in the healthcare AI context; examples
  from public health AI programs that have done this well (or badly).
- **Patient-facing AI tools and disclosure**: what patients should be told
  when they interact with an AI chatbot, AI symptom checker, or AI-generated
  message from their care team; the FTC's guidance on deceptive AI interfaces;
  the California AB 3030 requirement for AI disclosure in patient communications
  (in effect January 2025); how to write disclosure language that is
  informative without being alarming.
- **Trust recovery**: what to do when an AI system harms a patient or a
  community's trust is damaged by an adverse AI event; the incident
  communication approach; the role of transparency reports.
- **Equity and the access dimension of trust**: trust is lower in communities
  with less access to high-quality care; AI deployed in those communities
  without community input can deepen the distrust gap. What equitable AI
  deployment looks like in an AMC that serves a diverse population.

## Figure and table targets

- A trust survey summary figure or table: demographic groups × AI application
  types × trust levels, drawn from published survey data.
- A Patient AI Advisory Council charter table: membership, role, relationship
  to AISC, types of decisions they are consulted on vs. have authority over.
- A Mermaid diagram of the community engagement process for a new AI
  deployment: identify affected communities → engage early (before deployment,
  not after) → co-design disclosure language → pilot with community feedback
  loop → publish outcomes.

## Open questions to flag

- California AB 3030 (AI disclosure in patient communications): has it been
  challenged or amended since enactment? Are other states following?
- The Patient AI Advisory Council model: find the most specific and documented
  examples from AMCs or health systems. This is an emerging structure and
  concrete examples are scarce; flag where the literature is thin.
- The empirical literature on whether higher disclosure/transparency actually
  improves patient trust and outcomes: this is more nuanced than assumed.
